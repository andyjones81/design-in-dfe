Principles

Our design principles demonstrate our commitment to good design. They empower us to design the right things, the right way, consistently.

Be accessible from the start

Accessibility must never be an afterthought or a task to do once you’ve got MVP out the door. You should be considering how you ensure services are accessible right from the start and throughout the design and delivery lifecycle.

Designers creating user interactions and writing content, must consider who may be using the service, or may do in the future. If you know the service has a low number of disabled users than the national average, this may change in future, don’t just design for now.

Allowing our users to use services which are not accessible, makes the service harder to use, wastes time and money fixing problems in the future, and exposes us to unnecessary risk.

Design consistently

Everything you are looking to design has probably already been designed elsewhere already, so make use of existing patterns and components.

You should aim to design to established, industry best practices - users spend most of their time on other sites, so users will expect our services to work the same way as other sites they already use.

Using and improving existing GOV.UK Design System components, enables you to create services which will feel familiar to users who use other government services.

When designs behave the same, users won’t have to learn something new, they know what to expect based on a familiar experience. Users will trust a service that feels familiar, even if what they are doing is nothing like another service or task they do elsewhere.

Design for less

Start designing with just content. Iterate design to make it more usability, not more complex.

Overloading a page with components and different elements, can make it overwhelming and cause cognitive overload. Make best use of whitespace and headers to make things easier to understand, navigate and use.

Some things in design are functional, others purely aesthetic. If they aren’t adding to the overall experience, then remove it - reduce the design to the necessary features for the user to complete their task.

When writing content, write only what you need to. Make the main thing on a page, the most important thing the user needs to do or understand.

Design with context

You should be designing service interactions, touchpoints and content based on knowlege of how and where the users of the service will use them. This is a key principle, applied through Service Standard at Point 1 - Understand users and their needs

Put the most important or frequent thing a user needs to do, high up on a page, or make it the main thing on the page.

Understanding users’ circumstances, culture, language, abilities and capabilities, helps us to design services and content that works for everyone.

This doesn’t only enable us to design inclusive services, it also enables us to mitigate biases we may design into our service unintentionally.

Provide information and functionality in context - at the point the user is most likely to need it. Save the user from having to hunt for it, or missing it altogether.

Write for your audience

Content design makes things easier for people to understand and use. Using data, research and testing to understand what people need and then design content to meet those needs.

Write in plain English and explain complex words and abbreviations.

Consider what your words mean or how they can be interpreted or translated, think about users whose first language isn’t English.

Make it efficient

The best user experience comes from services that react quickly, prevent mistakes and enable users to do what they need to, quickly.

Friction in services makes things harder to use, understand and cause frustration.

Think about how your design works for different user groups. Some users may be novice so need extra help, others proficient. Can you give users shortcuts to make their use of the same service easier?

Avoid making service interactions complicated, its better to spread a process out over multiple pages rather than put a lot of things on one page.

Do the hard work to make it simple. This applies for the user’s benefit, but also iterating design and content. Its much easier to change a simple page, than one that has lots of elements or logic on it, or where it appears in a journey.
